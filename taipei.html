MON JUN 29 — Teeny time in Taipei

5 stops

The trip begins with an overnight EVA Air flight landing in Taipei on Monday morning. The connection to Chengdu leaves in the evening, which opens a real window: a half-day in one of Asia's most livable cities. The MRT from Taoyuan airport reaches the city in about 35 minutes, and the route is a gentle one. Start with incense and fortune-telling at Longshan Temple in the city's oldest quarter, walk to the Botanical Garden's lotus ponds, then settle in around Taipei Main Station, whose underground food halls could swallow an afternoon on their own. If time and energy hold, Dadaocheng Wharf and the old tea-merchant street leading to it make a fine last look at the city. Allow at least 2.5 hours from the city back to Taoyuan for international check-in.

Bangka Lungshan Temple 艋舺龍山寺

Religious Site
Hours: 06:00–21:45Suggested time: 45 min–1 hrFrom previous: ~50 min from airport: Taoyuan Airport MRT to Taipei Main, then Blue Line two stops to Longshan Temple Station
Address: No. 211, Guangzhou St, Wanhua District, Taipei 108
Coordinates: 25.0371, 121.4999
Taipei's most beloved temple, founded in 1738 by Fujian settlers and rebuilt after earthquakes, typhoons, and a direct hit from Allied bombing in 1945. Buddhist, Taoist, and folk deities share the courtyards: Guanyin in the main hall, Mazu behind her, and in the rear courtyard Yue Lao, the old man under the moon who ties destined couples together with red thread. Watch locals drop the crescent-shaped jiaobei blocks to ask their questions.
Pro Tips Go early; the morning chanting service and the incense smoke in slanted light are the best of it. As newlyweds, paying respects to Yue Lao, the matchmaker god, is the obvious move. The surrounding Bangka quarter is the oldest part of the city.

Taipei Botanical Garden 台北植物園

Park
Hours: 05:30–22:00Suggested time: 45 min–1 hrFrom previous: ~20 min walk from Longshan Temple, or one MRT stop to Xiaonanmen Station
Address: No. 53, Nanhai Rd, Zhongzheng District, Taipei 100
Coordinates: 25.0324, 121.5094
Twenty green acres laid out under Japanese rule in 1896, now an island of quiet a few blocks from the government district. The lotus pond is the centerpiece and early July is peak bloom, with night herons stalking the shallows. Tucked inside the grounds stands the Qing Imperial Envoy Hall (欽差行臺), the last surviving Qing government building in Taiwan, relocated here and easy to miss.
Pro Tips Enter from the Nanhai Road gate and circle the lotus pond first. July mornings are hot; the banyan-shaded paths on the west side are the cool route through.

A good place to eat well, cool off, and stage luggage logistics before the airport run.

Where Taipei got rich: the riverside port that boomed on the 1860s tea trade. Pro Tips This is the flex stop. Skip it without guilt if the day ran long; if you go, one drink at the containers as the light drops, then taxi straight back for the bags. Do not cut the 2.5 hour airport buffer.

Read before Taipei

Through the eras

before 1600

Taiwan's first six thousand years belong to its Indigenous peoples, and through them to half the planet: linguists trace the whole Austronesian family, the languages of the Philippines, Indonesia, Polynesia, Hawai'i, even Madagascar, back to this one island. The great Pacific voyaging tradition you flew over to get here began, most likely, on Taiwan's east coast. Sixteen recognized Indigenous peoples remain today, about 2.5 percent of the population. Portuguese sailors passing in the 1540s recorded the name that stuck for centuries: Ilha Formosa, beautiful island.

1700s–1895

The frontier centuries. Migrant families crossed the strait from Fujian, Hoklo and Hakka farmers carving rice paddies and tea terraces out of the hills, trading camphor and sugar through new ports. Taipei itself is young: the basin was settled around 1709, the walled city went up only in the 1880s, and in 1885 Taiwan became a full Qing province with Taipei as capital, exactly one decade before it was handed to someone else.

1945–1987

The Republic of China took Taiwan back in 1945; within two years the February 28 Incident of 1947, an island-wide uprising crushed with thousands killed, opened four decades of martial law remembered as the White Terror. In 1949, having lost the civil war to Mao, Chiang Kai-shek's government retreated here with well over a million soldiers and refugees, plus the imperial art treasures now in the National Palace Museum. Taipei became the 'temporary' capital of a government that still claimed all of China, a claim frozen in place for a generation.

✓Understand Taipei — the gentle first step▾

Taipei is the soft landing. You step off a thirteen-hour flight into a basin city ringed by green volcanic mountains, where night markets hum below glass towers and the metro is so polite it feels choreographed. The city runs on Mandarin, like the mainland ahead of you, but English signage is everywhere, Google still works, and strangers will walk you to the thing you are looking for. A half day here covers the itinerary's gentle loop: Longshan Temple, the Botanical Garden's lotus ponds, and the food halls around Taipei Main Station, with Dadaocheng Wharf as the bonus round if energy holds. Treat it as the decompression chamber between Los Angeles and Chengdu, and let the jet lag burn off over a bowl of beef noodle soup.

✓Traditional characters: reading the older script▾

Taiwan never simplified its writing. The mainland streamlined thousands of characters in the 1950s and 60s, but here the full traditional forms (繁體字) survive on every sign. The same word can look quite different: love is 愛 in Taipei and 爱 across the strait, and the traditional form famously keeps its heart (心) in the middle. Dragon is 龍 here, 龙 there. The spoken language is the same Mandarin, so everything you practice out loud in Taipei keeps working for the rest of the trip; the script just gets simpler as you go.

✓Night markets and the small-eats philosophy▾

Taipei eats in motion. The city perfected xiaochi, small eats: instead of one restaurant meal, you graze across a dozen stalls at markets like Shilin (士林) or Raohe (饒河街). CRITICAL for us: peanut is woven through Taipei street food more than anywhere else on this trip. Peanut powder (花生粉) is dusted over mochi, rolled into runbing wraps, and shaved in ribbons over ice cream burritos; peanut brittle hides in desserts that look innocent. And the night-market signature, the oyster omelet (蚵仔煎), is strictly off limits for Amanda. Safe grazing: grilled meat skewers, scallion pancakes (confirm the griddle), sweet potato balls, and cut fruit. Ask, every stall, every time.

✓Longshan Temple: 288 years of asking nicely▾

Bangka Lungshan Temple (艋舺龍山寺) has anchored Taipei's oldest quarter since 1738, when settlers from Fujian carried their home temple's incense across the strait. It has been flattened and rebuilt repeatedly, by earthquake in 1815, typhoon in 1867, and American bombs in 1945, and each rebuild made it more loved. Inside, the lines between religions blur the way they do all over Taiwan: Guanyin, the Buddhist bodhisattva of mercy, holds the main hall while Mazu the sea goddess and a full bench of folk deities work the back courtyards. Watch for worshippers dropping crescent moon blocks (jiaobei) to get yes-or-no answers, and find Yue Lao, the matchmaker god who ties couples together with invisible red thread. You two arrive pre-tied; a thank-you is traditional.

✓The Botanical Garden and the leftover Qing yamen▾

The Taipei Botanical Garden is the Japanese era at its most benign: a research garden laid out in 1896, the year after Japan took the island, and still run by the forestry institute that grew out of it. Twenty acres of banyans, palms, and bamboo wrap around a lotus pond that peaks in exactly the week you visit; July mornings there are all pink blooms and night herons. The quiet sleeper is in the trees on the garden's edge: the Qing Imperial Envoy Hall (欽差行臺), the last Qing government building left in Taiwan, moved here board by board when the Japanese cleared the old walled city. One garden holds the whole handover in miniature.

✓Dadaocheng: the tea port that built Taipei▾

Before Taipei was a capital it was a tea port. After 1860 the Tamsui River opened to foreign trade and Dadaocheng (大稻埕) boomed, shipping oolong to New York and London while its merchants built the baroque shophouses that still line Dihua Street, the best-preserved old street in the city. The trade is still real: dried mushrooms, herbs, karasumi, and bolts of fabric move out of the same arcades, now interleaved with coffee bars and design shops. The street ends at the wharf where the cargo once loaded, today a row of container bars facing the sunset. If the schedule allows it, this is the right last image of Taipei: the old river view, drink in hand, plane in a few hours.

✓Koxinga and the contested 1600s▾

The 1600s turned Taiwan into a chessboard. The Dutch East India Company built Fort Zeelandia in the south in 1624 while Spain briefly held the north near modern Taipei; the Dutch evicted the Spanish, then were evicted themselves in 1662 by Koxinga (鄭成功), a half-Japanese Ming loyalist admiral fleeing the new Qing dynasty. His family kingdom lasted one generation before the Qing took the island in 1683. Koxinga is now claimed as a hero by nearly everyone: Ming loyalist, Taiwanese founding father, even a deity with temples in his name.

✓Fifty years Japanese (1895–1945)▾

China ceded Taiwan to Japan in 1895 after losing the First Sino-Japanese War, and the island became Japan's first colony, its showcase. Railways, public health campaigns, universities, and Taipei's street grid date from these five decades; so do Ximending, the Red House, and the Presidential Office building. The memory is genuinely mixed, modernization braided with forced assimilation and wartime conscription, which is why Taipei today feels so strikingly comfortable with Japan: the food, the department stores, the politeness culture all carry the imprint.

✓The economic miracle▾

Out of that hard era came one of the Four Asian Tigers. Land reform, export factories, and ferocious schooling turned a poor agricultural island into an electronics powerhouse; by the 1980s Taiwan made the world's calculators, then its motherboards, then, through TSMC (founded 1987), the advanced semiconductors nearly every phone and AI chip on Earth now depends on. It is not a stretch to say the device you are reading this on exists because of decisions made within a few miles of your hotel.

✓Democracy, loudly (1987–today)▾

Martial law lifted in 1987, and Taiwan democratized at a sprint: first direct presidential election in 1996, first peaceful transfer of power in 2000, and since then a rotation of parties, a raucous free press, and a legislature famous for the occasional shoving match. In 2019 it became the first place in Asia to legalize same-sex marriage. Whatever metric you pick, Taipei now anchors one of the freest societies in Asia, and the civic pride in that is palpable.

✓Across the strait: a traveler's tact▾

Practical notes for the layover. Taiwan is a separate entry with its own currency, the New Taiwan Dollar (NT$), so do not expect mainland apps to behave the same: here Google Maps works beautifully, no VPN needed, and your credit cards tap almost everywhere. Enjoy that for a day; it changes in Chengdu. On politics: Taiwan's status is the most sensitive topic in the region. Locals on both sides of the strait are warm hosts, and the easy rule is that prickly subjects are theirs to raise, never ours. You will feel the contrasts yourself within 24 hours, which is more instructive than any briefing.
