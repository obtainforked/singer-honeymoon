A four-thousand-year throughline

China is the world's oldest continuous civilization, a single cultural thread running unbroken from Bronze Age dynasties to today's superpower. For most of recorded history it was among the richest, most populous and most technologically advanced societies on earth: it gave the world paper, printing, gunpowder, the compass, porcelain and the civil-service exam. Power was organized around dynasties, ruling houses that rose, peaked, decayed and were replaced in a pattern Chinese historians called the 'dynastic cycle,' legitimized by the 'Mandate of Heaven.' A Confucian scholar-bureaucracy, chosen by brutal written examinations, actually ran the empire. Holding that depth and continuity in mind is what makes the violent ruptures of the last two centuries, which shaped almost everything you'll see, so dramatic.

天命'Mandate of Heaven' (tiānmìng) — the idea that just rulers hold a divine right that unjust ones forfeit.

The 'Century of Humiliation' (1839–1949)

This is the single most important frame for understanding modern China, and it runs straight through this trip. For centuries China saw itself as the 'Middle Kingdom,' the civilized center of the world. That self-image shattered when Britain, hungry for tea and angry at China's refusal to open trade, flooded the country with Indian opium and then won the Opium Wars (from 1839) when China tried to stop it. The defeats forced a series of 'unequal treaties': Hong Kong was ceded to Britain, treaty ports like Guangzhou, Shanghai and Chongqing were opened to foreign control, and foreigners gained extraterritorial rights on Chinese soil. Over the following century came more invasions, the carving of 'spheres of influence' by European powers and Japan, internal rebellions that killed tens of millions, and finally the catastrophic Japanese invasion of 1937–45. Nearly every site on this trip, the colonial cathedrals and arcades, the treaty-port islands, the war museums, the very existence of Hong Kong and Macau as separate places, traces to this era. The determination to never be humiliated again is one of the deepest drivers of China's modern ambition.

百年国耻'Century of Humiliation' (bǎinián guóchǐ) — the era of foreign domination, 1839–1949.

Revolution & the founding of modern China (1911–1949)

In 1911 a revolution toppled the Qing, the last dynasty, ending more than two thousand years of imperial rule. Sun Yat-sen, revered today on both sides of the strait as the 'father of the nation' and based largely in Guangzhou, became the founding figure of the new republic. But the republic was fragile: real power fractured among regional warlords, and the 1920s–40s became a long, bloody contest between the Nationalists (KMT) under Chiang Kai-shek and the rising Communist Party under Mao Zedong, interrupted by the all-consuming Japanese invasion, during which Chongqing served as the besieged wartime capital. When Japan was defeated in 1945 the civil war resumed, and in 1949 Mao's Communists won. He declared the People's Republic from Beijing; Chiang's Nationalists fled to Taiwan, the root of a division still unresolved today.

孙中山Sun Yat-sen — revered as the founding father of modern China; closely tied to Guangzhou.

Mao's China & the road to reform (1949–1978)

The first three decades of the People's Republic were turbulent and often tragic. Mao reunified and stabilized a war-ravaged country and reasserted its independence, but his radical campaigns caused immense suffering: the Great Leap Forward (1958–62), a forced industrialization-and-collectivization drive, triggered a famine that killed tens of millions, and the Cultural Revolution (1966–76) plunged the country into a decade of political chaos, persecution and destruction of cultural heritage. This era is handled cautiously and selectively in China's own museums, you'll see far more about the imperial past and the post-1978 boom than about these years. Understanding that they happened, and that they ended only with Mao's death in 1976, is essential context for the explosive change that followed.

改革开放'Reform and Opening' (gǎigé kāifàng) — Deng's post-1978 modernization drive.

Reform, opening & the rise (1978–today)

After Mao's death, Deng Xiaoping launched the 'Reform and Opening' that created the China you'll travel through. He embraced market economics, foreign investment and private enterprise while keeping one-party political control. 'socialism with Chinese characteristics.' The Special Economic Zones (Shenzhen first, in 1980) were the laboratory. The results were staggering: the fastest mass escape from poverty in human history, hundreds of new cities, the world's largest high-speed rail network, and a leap to the technological frontier in payments, EVs, drones and AI. The bullet trains, cashless QR codes, and futuristic skylines you'll move through are barely 40 years old, built at breakneck speed on top of that very long history. China today is again one of the world's two great powers, which gives the whole 'Century of Humiliation' narrative its emotional charge.

改革开放'Reform and Opening' (gǎigé kāifàng) — Deng's post-1978 turn to markets that built modern China.

The land: scale, geography & the south

China is roughly the size of the United States but with over four times the people, spanning deserts, the Tibetan plateau, and subtropical coast. This trip stays entirely in the warm, wet, fertile south, first the Sichuan basin (a mountain-ringed bowl of 100+ million people, foggy and lush), then the Pearl River Delta on the southern coast, one of the densest and most economically powerful urban regions on earth, fusing Guangzhou, Shenzhen, Hong Kong, Macau and more into a single megalopolis. The south has always been China's outward-facing, trading, food-obsessed half, distinct in language, cuisine and temperament from the northern heartland around Beijing.

Food: the regional map

There's no single 'Chinese food', there are great regional traditions, and this trip hits two of the most famous. Sichuan cuisine (Chengdu, Chongqing) is bold, oily and built on the 'numbing-spicy' málà of chilies and Sichuan peppercorn, hot pot is its temple. Cantonese cuisine (Guangzhou, Hong Kong) is the opposite: subtle, fresh, technique-driven, prizing the natural flavor of top ingredients, the home of dim sum, roast meats and seafood. Hainan adds tropical and seafood dishes; Macau layers in Portuguese-Chinese fusion. Eating is central to the trip, just navigate it carefully given the allergies (see the Prep page).

麻辣 / 粤菜málà (numbing-spicy, Sichuan) vs. yuècài (Cantonese cuisine) — the two poles of this trip's food.

Belief: the three teachings & folk religion

China's spiritual life blends Confucianism (an ethical-social philosophy of order, hierarchy and family), Daoism (a native religion of balance, nature and the Dao), and Buddhism (imported from India ~2,000 years ago), alongside deep ancestor veneration and a crowded folk pantheon of local gods. People often draw on all of them without contradiction. You'll see this everywhere on the trip, incense-hazed temples like Man Mo in Hong Kong, Daoist sites, mosques marking China's old Silk Road and maritime trade with the Muslim world, and grand cathedrals from the colonial era. China is officially atheist, but everyday religious practice is widespread and visible.
