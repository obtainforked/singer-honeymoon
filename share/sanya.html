🏝 Home base
Banyan Tree Sanya Resort & Spa
📍 Luling Rd, Luhuitou Bay

This is the honeymoon's true rest stop, an all-villa resort on Luhuitou Bay, on Sanya's quieter southern coast, each villa with its own private pool set around a tropical lagoon on a 13-hectare estate. It's an oceanfront hideaway built for exactly this: slow mornings, the spa, the private beach, dinner without leaving the grounds. Luhuitou Park (the famous deer-turning-its-head viewpoint over the bay) is a five-minute walk, and downtown Sanya and Dadonghai beach are a short drive, but the point of these days is to not go far. Expect quiet, green, and pampered.

SUN JUL 5 — The honey part of the moon

2 stops
Phrase of the day海滩hǎitān“high-tahn”beach

A 1:20 PM landing gives you the afternoon. Transfer to Banyan Tree (about 30 minutes by taxi), check in, and you have a proper beach afternoon ahead. The days that follow have no locked structure. The stops here are a menu: the rainforest park, the outdoor show, the coral island, the hilltop lookouts. Do any combination across however many days feels right, or none of them and just use the beach. Two things worth booking in advance if there is any chance you want them: the Wuzhizhou Island ferry (daily visitor cap) and the Red Detachment of Women show (sells out).

Banyan Tree Sanya sits on a private stretch of Luhuitou Bay, quieter and more secluded than the main Yalong Bay resort strip. The resort is structured around standalone pool villas: your Deluxe Lagoon View villa has a private pool, open-air rain shower, and direct views over the lagoon toward the South China Sea. MON JUL 6 — Bing Chilling

3 stops
Phrase of the day冰淇淋bīngqílín“bing-chee-lin”ice cream — yes, that meme

Yalong Bay Tropical Paradise Forest Park is the area's best morning option: a hillside rainforest above the bay with glass walkways, suspension bridges, orchid gardens, and sweeping views down to the water. Go early to beat the heat and the tour groups. Some sections (the rafting run, the Canghai Tower) have been intermittently closed, so check the day of. The Red Detachment of Women show is a Sanya signature worth considering any evening it's running: an outdoor revolutionary epic with 3D effects, live fire, water, and dynamic rotating seating. Book ahead if you want it; it sells out. If Chunyuan Seafood Square comes up for dinner, it's a pick-your-own seafood market with high cross-contamination risk, a genuine no-go for Amanda's shellfish allergy.

TUE JUL 7 — Island time

2 stops
Phrase of the day干杯gānbēi“gahn-bay”cheers!

Two main options for this stretch of the stay. Wuzhizhou Island, off Haitang Bay, is widely considered the most beautiful island in Hainan: coral reefs, white sand, clear water, and a hilltop pagoda trail. A daily visitor cap means the ferry sells out, so book ahead. Once there, how you spend it is up to you: snorkeling, diving, the trails, or just the beach. Reef-safe sunscreen is required on the island to protect the coral. Luhuitou Park is the hilltop lookout above Sanya Bay, best in late afternoon when the light is warm. Both work well together in a day, or either one stands alone if you want to go slow.

1

Luhuitou Park (optional) 鹿回头公园

Park
Hours: 08:00–22:30Suggested time: 1–1.5 hrFrom previous: ~20 min taxi
Address: Luling Rd, Dadonghai, Sanya
The hilltop park above the resort, named for the legend of a deer that turned its head and became a maiden. The summit sits 902 feet up with the best panoramic view of Sanya Bay and the city. The deer statue at the top is the city's emblem. It's also a five-minute walk from the Banyan Tree gate, which makes it your easiest outing of the whole stay.
Pro Tips Go late afternoon and stay for sunset over the bay. The walk up takes 20 to 30 minutes, or there's a buggy. This one needs zero planning: out the resort gate and up.
"Split opinion - free entry but the forced sightseeing cart (¥38) angers many. 'If you can walk, take the free path - the park is small.' Beautiful sunset views. Go for sunset; skip the cart."

Wuzhizhou Island (Lovers' Island) (optional) 蝓支洲岛

Address: Haitang Bay, Haitang District (3.4 miles off pier)
A small teardrop island off Haitang Bay with the clearest water in Hainan, the best coral, and a daily visitor cap. Snorkeling, diving, sea-walking, glass-bottom boats. The 'Lovers' Island' branding is heavy-handed but the water genuinely is that blue.
Pro Tips Book ferry tickets online ahead; the cap means weekend sellouts. Be at the pier before 9 AM for the calmest water and best light. Reef-safe sunscreen only. ⚠️ Allergy note for any boat-day snacks: pack your own. Island food service is seafood-centric.
"'Hainan's most beautiful island' - crystal-clear water. Daily visitor cap - book ferry online ahead. Reach pier before 9 AM. Reef-safe sunscreen required. Avoid seafood on-island (shellfish risk). ¥144 ticket + ferry."

WED JUL 8 — A few hours in Haikou

4 stops
Phrase of the day卧铺wòpù“wor-poo”sleeper berth
Sleeper train
HAIKOU EAST → GUANGZHOU
Z502 overnight sleeper · 8:20 PM – 8:05 AM (+1) · soft sleeper
Your train boards a ship tonight: the Qiongzhou Strait rail ferry

The structure here is fixed: check out, take the 1:20 PM train from Sanya to Haikou (arrives 3:01 PM), spend a few hours in Hainan's capital, and board the overnight sleeper from Haikou East at 8:20 PM. What you do in Haikou is up to you. The Hainan Provincial Museum is the most worthwhile stop, its Li and Miao ethnic-culture galleries are genuinely exceptional and nothing like the resort coast. Qilou Old Street, the arcaded Nanyang shophouse district, is best in the early evening when the buildings are lit. You don't need both. The sleeper itself is the highlight: no bridge or tunnel crosses the Qiongzhou Strait, so the entire train is loaded onto a ferry and floated across to the mainland, one of the last crossings of its kind. Settle into the berth and you wake up in Guangdong.

Haikou's arcaded shophouse district, built in the early 1900s by Hainanese who made money in Southeast Asia and came home. Read before Sanya

Through the eras

Imperial era

For most of imperial history Hainan was the empire's farthest, wildest frontier, the end of the known world, where disgraced officials and exiles were banished. Its earliest inhabitants, the Li people, had lived on the island for over 3,000 years, long predating Han Chinese settlement.

19th–20th c.

A poor, malarial agricultural island through the colonial era and most of the 20th century, largely bypassed by the upheavals and development on the mainland, remote, rural, and overlooked.

Today

Transformed in a single generation: made a province and Special Economic Zone in 1988, Hainan was deliberately developed into China's tropical resort playground, the 'Hawaii of China.' The Sanya of luxury beach resorts and duty-free malls you'll see is essentially a creation of the last 30 years.

✓Understand Sanya — China's tropical south▾

Sanya sits at the southern tip of Hainan, a province that is itself an island, which makes this the closest thing China has to a domestic Hawaii, and it's marketed exactly that way. The latitude (around 18°N) puts it firmly in the tropics: palms, monsoon humidity, and water warm enough to swim in year-round. The city is organized around its bays rather than a single downtown. Dadonghai is the closest, most built-up beach strip; Yalong Bay to the east is the resort enclave (your Banyan Tree territory), a curve of white sand backed by five-star compounds; Sanya Bay runs long and casual along the city's western edge. Offshore, Wuzhizhou Island draws the snorkel-and-dive crowd. Two things to understand as a traveler: first, Sanya runs on domestic tourism, it's where the rest of China comes for sun, so expect Mandarin and Russian long before English, and peak-season crowds. Second, the local food logic is seafood-first and coconut-everything, which for this trip means the pick-your-own seafood markets are a minefield to navigate carefully rather than a highlight to chase.

✓Hainan: China's tropical south▾

Hainan is China's southernmost province, a tropical island in the South China Sea on roughly the same latitude as Hawaii. For most of history it was the remote frontier where officials were exiled; today it's the country's beach-resort escape, with Sanya as its glossy capital of palm-lined bays and warm, clear water. After the intensity of Chongqing, this is the deliberate slow stretch of the trip.

✓Hainan: China's island free-trade experiment▾

Sanya sits at the southern tip of Hainan, which is more than beach province but the site of China's boldest economic experiment since Shenzhen. The whole island is being built into the Hainan Free Trade Port, and in December 2025 it reached a milestone: 'full-island customs closure,' meaning Hainan now operates as a special customs zone separate from the mainland. Goods from abroad enter the island largely tariff-free, while a 'second line' of customs control sits between Hainan and the rest of China. Beijing's stated ambition is a rival to Hong Kong and Singapore. For a visitor it explains the duty-free megamalls, and a practical catch worth remembering: what you buy duty-free in Hainan crosses a real customs line if you carry it onward to the mainland.

✓Li & Miao indigenous cultures▾

Long before resorts, Hainan was home to the Li people, the island's earliest inhabitants (here over 3,000 years), and later the Miao. The Li are renowned for intricate brocade weaving and, historically, facial tattooing that marked lineage and identity. Their cultures predate Han Chinese settlement of the island by millennia, the provincial museum in Haikou has the best window into this heritage if you want depth beyond the beaches.

✓The Li and Miao: Hainan's first peoples▾

Long before Han Chinese settlement, Hainan was home to the Li, who have lived on the island for roughly 3,000 years, joined later by the Miao. The Li developed sophisticated cotton-weaving and distinctive facial-tattoo traditions, and their textile techniques influenced one of the most important figures in Chinese textile history. Their villages, crafts, and language survive in the island's mountainous interior, and the Hainan Provincial Museum's ethnographic galleries are the best window into this indigenous culture.

✓Coconuts, betel nut, and tropical culture▾

Hainan's tropical setting gives it a food and daily culture unlike anywhere else in China, coconut in everything (rice, chicken, sweets), the qingbuliang cooling dessert, betel-nut chewing, and Hainan-style chicken rice that emigrants carried abroad to become a Southeast Asian icon. The island's overseas connections run deep: generations of Hainanese emigrated across Southeast Asia, and returnees shaped Haikou's distinctive arcaded shophouse streets.

✓The edge of the empire: a place of exile▾

For most of imperial history, Hainan was the end of the known world, the remote tropical frontier where the Chinese state banished disgraced officials and scholars. The most famous, the Song-dynasty poet Su Dongpo, was exiled here around 1097 and, rather than despairing, taught, wrote, and is credited with helping bring mainland learning to the island. Haikou's Wugong Temple honors five such exiled officials. This 'end of the earth' identity is baked into Sanya's old name, Tianya Haijiao. 'the edge of the sky, the corner of the sea.'

✓A naval frontier on the South China Sea▾

Hainan has long been strategically pivotal as China's gateway to the South China Sea. Ming-dynasty fleets provisioned here, trade routes to Southeast Asia passed close by, and in the modern era the island anchors China's southern maritime presence. Sanya hosts a major naval base, and the city administers China's claims across the vast, contested waters to the south. It's a reminder that this honeymoon beach town sits at one of Asia's most sensitive geopolitical seams.

✓From malarial backwater to resort coast▾

Within living memory Hainan was poor, malarial, and underdeveloped, a hardship posting. Its transformation is recent and deliberate: made a province and Special Economic Zone in 1988, then aggressively built up as 'China's Hawaii,' with Sanya's Yalong and Haitang bays lined with international resorts and duty-free megamalls. The tropical-paradise Sanya you'll experience is essentially a creation of the last three decades, layered over a much older and harder island story.

✓Reef & marine ecology▾

Hainan's waters hold some of China's healthiest coral reefs, and Wuzhizhou Island is the showcase, clear visibility, reef fish, and easy snorkeling. Reefs everywhere are under pressure from warming seas and tourism, so the usual rules matter: reef-safe sunscreen, don't touch or stand on coral, keep your distance from marine life. Amanda's shellfish allergy is worth a thought around the seafood markets too, even just handling.
