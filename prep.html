Why these citiesthe throughline | what ties the route together▾

Why these cities (the throughline)

These seven cities trace a single arc: from China's relaxed inland heartland out to its hyper-modern southern coast. Chengdu and Chongqing show you the old 'Land of Abundance' and the dramatic mountain-river interior; Sanya is the tropical exhale in the middle; then Guangzhou, Shenzhen, Hong Kong and Macau form the Pearl River Delta, the trading, manufacturing and financial engine that has made modern China, where 2,000 years of foreign contact meet the most futuristic cities on earth. Inland history first, coastal future last.

On the groundetiquette | allergies | passport | health▾

Etiquette & a few phrases

A few habits smooth everything over. Greetings are low-key, a nod and 'nǐ hǎo' (nee-how) go far. Tipping is generally not expected and can confuse. Queue, keep your voice down on transit, and accept business cards or change with a light two-handed gesture as a courtesy. Photograph people only with a smile-and-gesture ask. In temples and mosques, dress modestly and follow the locals' lead on shoes and silence. A handful of phrases, xièxie (thanks), duōshǎo qián (how much), méiyǒu (don't have / none), earn warmth everywhere.

Food safety & our allergies

We both react to peanuts and tree nuts; Amanda also to shellfish. Refined peanut OIL is usually fine, but cold-pressed/unrefined peanut oil (common for flavour) is risky, carry the Chinese allergen cards and ask about ingredients, not just 'any allergies?'. Sichuan and Cantonese kitchens use nuts and shellfish heavily.

Health, water & staying well

Drink bottled or boiled water only; avoid tap water and ice you're unsure of. Pack a personal kit: any prescriptions (in original packaging with a doctor's note), rehydration salts, anti-diarrheal, motion-sickness tabs, and plenty of sunscreen for Sanya. Check routine vaccinations are current. Summer in southern China is hot and very humid, hydrate hard, plan indoor/AC breaks midday, and watch for heat fatigue. Pharmacies (look for the green cross) are common for minor needs.

Always carry your passport

In mainland China your passport is a functional daily document, not just a travel item. You need it to: register at hotels (required by law), enter some museums (free admission is tied to ID), buy train tickets and board trains (the booking system links tickets to passport numbers), check in at airports, and pass through security at some larger attractions. Always have the original — a photo on your phone is not accepted in most of these situations. A small crossbody pouch worn under a jacket is the practical solution. Keep a photo of the data page backed up to iCloud as a fallback for non-official situations.

Weatherheat | rain | typhoon season | what to pack▾

The short version

Late June through mid-July is the height of summer across all seven cities. Hot everywhere, humid most places, rainy afternoons across inland China. Plan outdoor sightseeing before 11 AM or after 4 PM and bring an umbrella every day without exception.

City by city

Chengdu (Jun 29 – Jul 3) Highs 88–93°F. Hot, overcast, frequent afternoon showers. The Sichuan Basin traps haze; clear sky is rare. Start outdoor stops early.

Chongqing (Jul 3–5) One of China’s “Three Furnaces.” Highs 95–100°F with punishing humidity. Afternoon thunderstorms are routine. Schedule museums and air-conditioned stops for midday.

Sanya (Jul 5–8) Tropical. Highs 90–93°F with high UV and sea humidity. Afternoon storms clear quickly. Beach time is best in the morning and late afternoon.

Guangzhou & Shenzhen (Jul 9–13) Highs 91–95°F. Heavy but brief afternoon downpours most days. Humidity is extreme. Early starts, shade at midday.

Hong Kong & Macau (Jul 12–18) Highs 88–91°F. July is typhoon season. The Hong Kong Observatory issues T-signals: T1 is a watch, T3 a precaution, T8 closes most businesses and reduces MTR service. Download the HKO app and check it before outdoor plans.

What to wear and bring

Moisture-wicking or linen clothing — cotton stays wet and heavy. Compact umbrella or packable rain jacket every single day. High-SPF sunscreen; reef-safe formula required on Wuzhizhou Island. A sun hat for any extended outdoor time. A light layer for indoors: AC in Chinese malls, trains, and restaurants is aggressive. Drink bottled water constantly.

Before you gochecklist | offline backup▾

Before you fly: the checklist

Confirm passports are valid 6+ months beyond travel and check the current visa situation for mainland China (rules change, verify the latest for US citizens, including any transit-visa-free allowances). Hong Kong and Macau have separate entry rules from the mainland, so each border crossing is a real immigration checkpoint. Set up Alipay and WeChat Pay and link cards while you still have easy connectivity. Tell your banks your travel dates. Screenshot every hotel confirmation and the allergen cards so they work offline. Download offline maps and this guide's offline file.

Connectivity-free backup plan

Assume your phone could fail or the firewall could block you at the worst moment, and pre-load accordingly: this guide's single offline file, offline city maps (Amap or Apple Maps downloaded), screenshots of all bookings and the allergen cards, and the hotel addresses in Chinese characters to show a taxi driver. Keep a printed copy of the key confirmations and the allergen card in your daypack. Redundancy here is cheap insurance for a trip this dependent on apps.

Money & paymentsAlipay | WeChat Pay | cash | safety▾

Money, apps & getting around

Set up Alipay and WeChat Pay before leaving, both now bind foreign Visa/Mastercard and are how you pay for almost everything, including metros and small stalls. Carry a little cash for rural stops. Download offline maps in Amap (高德) or Maps.me; Google Maps does not work in the mainland.

Payments: going cashless the Chinese way

China runs on QR-code payments. Alipay and WeChat Pay are used for nearly everything, from taxis to street stalls. Both now accept linked foreign Visa/Mastercard, but set them up and verify they work before departure. Carry some cash (RMB) as a fallback, especially for small vendors and rural spots; Hong Kong and Macau use their own currencies (HKD, MOP/HKD widely accepted) and take cards and Octopus more readily. Notify banks of travel to avoid frozen cards. ATMs at airports/banks are the cheapest way to get cash.

Money-smart & safety notes

China is very safe for travelers, violent crime is rare and you can comfortably be out at night. Normal city caution applies for pickpockets in dense markets (Mong Kok, Chunxi Road). Keep a photo of your passport separate from the original. Tap water aside, the main 'risk' is logistical: a dead phone means no payments, maps, or tickets, so guard your battery and carry a power bank and a little backup cash. Tipping is not customary on the mainland; modest tipping appears in Hong Kong/Macau tourist settings.

Getting aroundrail | metro | DiDi | security | timezone▾

Trains & the high-speed rail

China's high-speed rail is superb and the backbone of this trip. Book through Trip.com or the official 12306 (Trip.com is easier in English). You'll need your passport to book and to collect/scan at the station, passport = your ticket. Arrive 45–60 min early: big stations are airport-sized with security screening. Our Haikou→Guangzhou leg is an overnight sleeper that famously rolls onto a ferry to cross the strait. Seat classes: second class is perfectly comfortable; business/first for long legs if you want space.

Getting around cities: metro, taxi, ride-hail

Every city on this trip has an excellent metro, cheap, fast, English signage, and buyable via QR or transit card. For taxis, use DiDi (China's Uber, available in English and inside Alipay) rather than hailing, so the destination and fare are handled in-app and there's no language friction. In Hong Kong, get an Octopus card for metro/buses/ferries and convenience stores. Chongqing's metro is an attraction in itself; in Guangzhou the Pearl River water buses are a scenic, cheap way to move.

Security screenings

China runs security checks at metro stations, high-speed rail stations, airports, and some tourist sites and museums. Bags go through an X-ray machine and you walk through a body scanner, similar to airport security but faster. At rail stations the process is mandatory for all passengers; keep your bag accessible and remove bottles. Some stations also scan ID or passports at the gate. A few landmarks, particularly in politically sensitive areas, add additional checks. It adds one to three minutes to any journey — factor this into connections, especially at busy stations during peak hours. Security staff are professional and the process is routine; just follow the person ahead of you.

One timezone across the whole country

China runs on a single timezone nationwide: UTC+8 (CST), even though the country spans five natural geographic zones. This means sunrise in Chengdu (our westernmost stop) is around 6:15 AM in late June, while it is fully light by 5:00 AM in Hong Kong. There is no daylight saving adjustment. All train and flight departure times are in Beijing time regardless of where you are. For us this is seamless since every city on the trip (including Hong Kong and Macau) runs UTC+8. One practical note: in Sanya, sunset is around 19:30 in early July — later than it feels given the latitude.

ConnectivityVPN | eSIM | the Great Firewall▾

Connectivity: VPN, eSIM & the Great Firewall

On the mainland, Google (Maps, Gmail, search), WhatsApp, Instagram, and many Western sites are blocked. Install and TEST a reputable VPN before you leave, you generally can't download one once you're inside. Better still, an international roaming eSIM (e.g. from your carrier or a travel-eSIM provider) routes through servers outside China and often sidesteps the firewall entirely, keeping your normal apps working. Hong Kong and Macau are uncensored, everything works there. Have a backup plan: VPNs can be unreliable, so don't depend on Google Maps; use Amap or Apple Maps offline.

Shoppingbrands · flagships · what to bring home▾

Worth packing for

China is genuinely worth shopping in, not just for souvenirs. Some categories here are cheaper than abroad and certain brands have no meaningful US presence at all. The ones to know: Chinese sportswear and cosmetics labels, duty-free luxury in Sanya, consumer electronics in Shenzhen, and local food gifts that are hard to replicate once you are home.

Li-Ning and Chinese sportswear

Li-Ning (李宁) was founded by Olympic gymnast Li Ning and is now one of China’s best-known labels. The sub-line China Li-Ning (中国李宁) is the one to focus on: runway-influenced streetwear with bold Chinese calligraphy graphics, released in limited colorways that rarely reach Western markets. The Chengdu Taikoo Li flagship is the best store on this route. Hoodies and tracksuits are the standout pieces. Anta (安踏) is China’s largest sportswear brand and the official Chinese Olympic team outfitter. Some national team edition pieces are domestic-only. Both brands run standard Chinese sizing; check the in-store guide.

Florasis and Chinese beauty

Florasis (花西子) makes cosmetics with intricate heritage-inspired packaging: carved lipsticks, engraved powder compacts, and hand-painted cases that look like artifacts. Hard to buy in the US without significant markup. Perfect Diary (完美日记) is on every shopping street and well-priced for the quality. Miniso (名创优品) carries stationery, home goods, and licensed IP collaborations (Sanrio, Disney, Pokémon) that sell out online. Everything costs roughly half the US retail price.

Xiaomi and Shenzhen electronics

Xiaomi (小米) Mi Stores are in every city on this route. Power banks, wireless earbuds, electric toothbrushes, and small gadgets run 30 to 50 percent less than equivalent US prices. The Huaqiangbei (华强北) electronics market in Shenzhen spans several buildings: cables, adapters, phone cases, components, and portable chargers at near-wholesale prices. Worth a walk-through even if you buy nothing.

Sanya: the world&rsquo;s largest duty-free mall

Haitang Bay International Duty-Free City sits about 20 minutes from central Sanya and covers over a million square feet. Major luxury houses alongside cosmetics counters, imported spirits, electronics, and accessories, all exempt from Chinese import duty under Hainan’s offshore free-trade policy. Shop any day during your stay and collect at the airport on departure. Best value on perfume, skincare, imported spirits, and anything from the big European houses. Bring your passport; purchases are logged against it. The Haitang Bay app lets you browse and pre-order.

City by city

Chengdu: Taikoo Li for flagship stores including Li-Ning. Chunxi Road for streetwear. The Research Base gift shop has high-quality panda goods. Pixian doubanjiang (郫县豆瓣酱) spice paste is the definitive Sichuan food souvenir. Check ingredients on spice packets; some contain peanuts.

Guangzhou: Beijing Road pedestrian street for general shopping. Shamian Island for antiques and curios. Cantonese chrysanthemum tea and aged tangerine peel (陈皮) make excellent gifts. Avoid the dried seafood counters.

Hong Kong: Cosmetics and spirits are duty-free. PMQ on Hollywood Road for local designers. Shanghai Tang (上海滩) on Pedder Street for premium Chinese-inspired fashion. For electronics, stick to verified chains (Fortress, Broadway) over individual Nathan Road shops.

Macau: Rua de S. Paulo below the Ruins is lined with Portuguese wine merchants, almond cookie shops, pork jerky stalls, and egg tart bakeries. Lord Stow’s at the Venetian is the original.

Food gifts and allergies

Chinese snack foods travel well and make strong gifts. Peanuts and tree nuts appear in many spice blends, mixed candies, and packaged snacks, so read labels. The allergen section on Chinese packaging is marked 过敗原 (guò mìn yuán). For Amanda: shellfish and shellfish-derived sauces appear in dried goods, soup bases, and some seasoning pastes. Safe categories: sealed teas, whole single-origin spices (Sichuan peppercorns are fine), and candy or snacks with a clear nut-free label.
